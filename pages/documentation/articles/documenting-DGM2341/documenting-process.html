To document or not to document? That is the question.

March 27, 2022

5 minute read

The importance of knowing all the technology you’ll be using in a project is key, albeit you may not be a master at everything, these fundamentals are still required for a designer and developer to know what technology is going into their project and why/how it works. Then, you also have to make sure you document your work and findings so that you can show them to your peers or future potential clients. I’ll be using some documentation I created that talks about codecs/formats and the technology behind them, it will serve as an example of what I think is the big question. To document or not to document?

As designers or developers, it is key to understand how each piece of technology weaves together to form a cohesive product. For example, a music streaming service would require a developer to understand each single file format and how it will impact the user. While a designer should still understand the fundamentals of these formats, they should be able to build a design around what a user would expect when they’re using their product. So, what is one way to make sure everyone is on the same page and understands the technology that they’re working with?

Document Everything!

Similar to the music streaming service example I provided, I had to research media formats of all kinds for a project. I can talk about how an image format may look depending on the format and compression setting being used, or I can explain how audio formats can impact a listener's experience. I wasn’t researching all this for a product, but the purpose was to gain this important knowledge of media formats and to learn how to properly document. You have to be able to tell an informative story when you are documenting anything, and to be able to present that story in a well-designed document.

There are a few key components to consider when you’re preparing to document something. You have to consider the goal of the document, the audience you’re reaching out to, and how you’ll structure a well-designed story about what you’ve learned. All of these components—and some practice—results in a document that is effective, informative, and can be beneficial for someone else down the line. For this documentation I’m showcasing, I considered all those components and it helped me structure my document.

Goal of the Document

The goal of your document can vary, you could be trying to explain the importance of responsive web design or how to properly implement logic into your JavaScript web application. For me, my goal was to research the digital media output and find a way to communicate that with a well-designed document. Before I started to communicate that though, I had to conduct research that was related to my goal. Much of the research was conducted by reading documentation and also by testing a variety of things. I did a full series of testing on image compression on the most common formats used on the web.

The Audience

When you start to craft your documentation, you have to figure out who or your audience is. This is important because if you write something that is specifically crafted for an audience, it could be more successful. For me, the example document I’m sharing was specifically to try to be informative, but without too much jargon. However, this document is by nature technical. This is fine because my audience consists of people in the digital media industry. But, if this was for a client and explained the difference between media files, I would be wise with my wording and find a way to make the document engaging. If you don’t consider your audience, you could potentially have a document that communicates information poorly.

The Document Structure

When you’re doing this kind of documentation, you have to consider how you’ll communicate any information to the reader/audience. For this, we should look towards any design principles and techniques that can help give the document some life and be more effective. Principles as simple as chunking relevant information is incredibly important for communication, but also using graphics that help paint a picture of what you want your reader to understand. Cool things to try are to use graphics that help the reader know where to look, see Fig X. for an example. The structure of the document is going to be the first thing the reader will notice and if it is too much noise or doesn’t have the proper flow, it could lead to confusion and failure of communicating to your audience.

Conclusion

To sum up the importance of documenting and the components I’ve presented. I believe that any designer or developer should document their work in a professional setting and research/personal projects. In a professional setting, documentation enables a company to have internal resources that can help the company in the long run. For research/personal projects, it’s great for you to document the journey you’re on. Doing this is great because it can help build bridges and open doors for you in the future, but we also live in a community of collaboration and the sharing of knowledge could help the industry grow.

The document I used as an example in this document will be free and available to look at. Again, it was for a research project about digital media formats and some other related topics. It may be overwhelming, and there may be errors you see that I didn’t catch. But I want to share my work and hopefully, someone else can look at it and get something out of it, either from the inspiration of how to have good documentation or some type of knowledge about digital media.
